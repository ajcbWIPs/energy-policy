# NEW POLICY FRAMEWORK SHOWS HOW AUSTRALIA CAN REPLACE $15.7B FUEL EXCISE WITH 4.35X ECONOMIC RETURNS

## Integrated energy efficiency and circular economy approach could create 100,000 jobs while reducing emissions

**SYDNEY, March 24, 2026** — A comprehensive policy framework released today demonstrates that Australia can not only replace declining fuel excise revenue but generate 4.35 times economic value through strategic investments in energy efficiency, electric mobility, and circular economy initiatives.

The framework, developed by independent policy researcher Andrew Baker, shows that every dollar of government subsidy can generate $4.35 in economic activity through job creation, health benefits, and productivity gains—more than compensating for the projected decline in fuel excise revenue as vehicle electrification accelerates.

### Key Findings:

**Economic Impact:**
- Current fuel excise revenue: $15.71 billion annually
- Replacement revenue achievable: $17.9 billion (114% replacement)
- Job creation potential: 100,000 full-time equivalents by 2030
- Average economic multiplier: 4.35x per subsidy dollar

**Energy Efficiency:**
- 35-45% reduction in domestic energy consumption achievable
- Waste heat recovery from appliances could power hot water systems
- Multi-cycle water systems reduce energy use by 60-70%

**Circular Economy:**
- 95% of vape battery materials can be recovered and reused
- Battery swapping infrastructure creates jobs while solving range anxiety
- Building on UNSW SMaRT Centre research led by Prof. Veena Sahajwalla

### The Challenge

Australian motorists paid an estimated $15.71 billion in net fuel excise in 2023-24, but this revenue stream is declining as electric vehicles gain market share. Current EV adoption stands at 12.4% nationally and accelerating.

"The decline in fuel excise revenue isn't a crisis—it's an opportunity," said Baker. "We can rebuild our economy on foundations of efficiency, circularity, and innovation while actually generating more value than the current system."

### The Solution

The framework proposes three integrated pillars:

**1. Building Efficiency Revolution**
- Mandatory waste heat recovery in new buildings
- Multi-cycle water systems that reuse greywater
- Thermal mass optimisation using recycled materials
- Target: 40% energy reduction by 2035

**2. Electric Mobility Infrastructure**
- Battery swapping stations every 5,000 residents in urban areas
- Protected e-bike lanes every 500m in city centers
- Vehicle-to-grid integration for grid stability
- Co-location with vape battery recycling points

**3. Circular Economy Scale-Up**
- Regional microfactories based on UNSW SMaRT Centre model
- Mandatory battery take-back at all retail points
- Transformation of construction waste into building materials
- Vape battery to powerbank conversion programs

### Policy Recommendations

**Immediate Actions (0-12 months):**
1. Establish Battery Stewardship Authority with $50M funding
2. Amend National Construction Code for waste heat recovery
3. Launch $500M EV Infrastructure Fund for battery swapping

**Medium-Term Reforms (1-3 years):**
1. Implement 15-minute city planning principles
2. Phase in distance-based road user charging
3. Expand TAFE programs for green skills training

**Long-Term Transformation (3-10 years):**
1. All buildings net-zero energy by 2040
2. 95% battery material recovery rate
3. 40% of urban trips by e-bike or walking

### Building on Australian Innovation

The framework specifically builds on Australian research excellence, particularly the work of Professor Veena Sahajwalla at UNSW's SMaRT Centre, who pioneered microfactory technology for transforming waste into high-value products.

"We have world-class research right here in Australia," said Baker. "This framework shows how to scale those innovations into economy-wide transformation."

### Addressing the Vape Battery Crisis

A unique feature of the framework is integration of vape battery recycling into the broader battery ecosystem. With 95% of materials recoverable from vape batteries, the framework proposes:
- Mandatory collection points at all battery swapping stations
- Regional processing using thermal transformation technology
- Output materials fed back into domestic battery manufacturing

Baker is submitting the framework to both NSW State Parliament and Federal Parliament, with requests for:
