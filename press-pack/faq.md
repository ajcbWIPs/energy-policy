# FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

## Australian Energy Efficiency & Circular Economy Framework
**Andrew Baker - March 2026**

---

## GENERAL QUESTIONS

### Q: What is this framework about?

**A:** This is a comprehensive policy framework showing how Australia can transform its economy by investing in energy efficiency, electric mobility, and circular economy initiatives. The key finding is that every dollar of government subsidy generates $4.35 in economic value—more than replacing declining fuel excise revenue while creating jobs and reducing emissions.

### Q: Who developed this framework?

**A:** Andrew Baker, an independent policy researcher. The framework incorporates data from Australian government agencies (ATO, ABS, AEMO, Clean Energy Regulator) and builds on Australian research excellence, particularly the work of Professor Veena Sahajwalla at UNSW's SMaRT Centre.

### Q: Why is this needed now?

**A:** Australia faces a critical juncture. Fuel excise revenue ($15.71 billion annually) is declining as electric vehicles gain market share (currently 12.4%). Rather than seeing this as a revenue crisis, we can use it as an opportunity to rebuild our economy on more sustainable, efficient, and valuable foundations.

## ECONOMIC QUESTIONS

### Q: How can you claim a 4.35x economic multiplier?

**A:** The multiplier is calculated using:
- **Direct effects:** Jobs and economic activity from the investment itself
- **Indirect effects:** Supply chain and induced spending
- **Health co-benefits:** Reduced healthcare costs from cleaner air and active transport
- **Environmental value:** Carbon reduction, waste diversion

For example, a $1,500 heat pump subsidy generates:
- $1,500 direct spending
- $2,400 indirect economic activity (supply chain, installation jobs)
- $1,800 health benefits (reduced gas emissions)
- $450 environmental benefits
- **Total: $6,150 value from $1,500 investment = 4.1x multiplier**

### Q: Won't this cost taxpayers money?

**A:** Initially, yes—there's an investment required ($4.45B over 10 years). But this generates:
- $17.9B in replacement revenue (114% of fuel excise)
- $2.3B in health system savings
- 100,000 jobs generating income tax
- Reduced welfare payments from employment

The framework actually **saves** money over time while creating value.

### Q: How do you replace fuel excise revenue?

**A:** Through a combination of:

**Direct Revenue ($8.3B):**
- Carbon pricing on remaining fossil fuels: $4.2B
- Road user charges for EVs: $3.8B
- Battery recycling levies: $0.3B

**Indirect Revenue Gains ($9.6B):**
- Income tax from new jobs: $2.1B
- GST from economic activity: $3.4B
- Company tax from new enterprises: $1.8B
- Health system savings: $2.3B

**Total: $17.9B vs. current $15.71B**

### Q: What about low-income households?

**A:** The framework includes specific provisions:
- Targeted subsidies for low-income home retrofits
- E-bike purchase programs for disadvantaged communities
- Job training programs in regional areas
- Energy bill reductions from efficiency (saving $800-1,200/year)

Low-income households typically spend 10-15% of income on energy. Efficiency measures can cut this by 40%, providing significant relief.

## TECHNICAL QUESTIONS

### Q: Is battery swapping viable in Australia?

**A:** Yes, with conditions:
- **Current status:** Pilot trials underway; Janus Electric operating truck swapping between Sydney-Brisbane (3-minute swaps)
- **Best use cases:** Urban areas, fleet vehicles, regional corridors
- **Challenges:** Battery standardisation, upfront infrastructure cost
- **Solutions:** Government incentives, OEM cooperation, open standards mandate

China has 2,000+ swapping stations; India has 500+. Australia can learn from these models while adapting to our geography.

### Q: How does waste heat recovery actually work?

**A:** Simple example:
- Your air conditioner removes heat from indoors and dumps it outside
- Instead of wasting that heat, we capture it with a heat exchanger
- The captured heat pre-heats your hot water
- Result: Your hot water system uses 40-60% less energy

Similar systems work for:
- Refrigerator condenser heat → space heating
- Clothes dryer exhaust → pre-heating ventilation air
- Shower greywater → heat pump source temperature

The technology exists and is proven in Europe and Japan.

### Q: Can you really recycle 95% of vape batteries?

**A:** Yes. Vape batteries contain:
- Lithium (recoverable for new batteries)
- Steel (construction materials)
- Graphite (battery anodes or soil amendment)
- Plastics (3D printing filament)

Companies like EcoSig Australia are already doing this, converting vapes to powerbanks. The B-cycle scheme accepts vape batteries. The framework scales this to national level with mandatory collection.

### Q: What's a microfactory?

**A:** Developed by Prof. Veena Sahajwalla at UNSW, microfactories are:
- Small-scale (fit in warehouse)
- Modular (can be replicated)
- Local (serve regional areas)
- Transform waste into high-value products

Examples:
- E-waste → 3D printing filament, circuit boards
- Plastics → Building materials, furniture
- Textiles → Insulation, acoustic panels
- Batteries → Lithium carbonate, steel

They create local jobs while solving waste problems.

## IMPLEMENTATION QUESTIONS

### Q: How long will this take to implement?

**A:** Three phases:

**Phase 1 (0-12 months):**
- Governance structures established
- Building code amendments
- Initial infrastructure funding
- Visible: Battery collection points, pilot swapping stations

**Phase 2 (1-3 years):**
- Urban planning reforms
- Skills training programs
- Infrastructure rollout
- Visible: E-bike lanes, swapping networks, retrofitted buildings

**Phase 3 (3-10 years):**
- Systemic transformation
- Net-zero buildings
- Circular economy at scale
- Visible: 40% active transport, 95% battery recovery

### Q: Who will pay for this?

**A:** Mix of sources:
- **Government:** $2.5B over 4 years (initial catalyst)
- **Private sector:** $1.95B (infrastructure, manufacturing)
- **Green bonds:** $1.5B (institutional investors)
- **Public-private partnerships:** Shared risk/reward

The $4.45B total investment generates $17.9B revenue—self-funding over time.

### Q: What happens if the technology changes?

**A:** The framework builds in flexibility:
- **Open standards mandate:** No technology lock-in
- **Regular reviews:** Every 2 years by independent experts
- **Modular design:** Can swap components without system overhaul
- **R&D funding:** 5% of budget for emerging technologies

Example: If solid-state batteries replace lithium-ion, the swapping infrastructure adapts; the recycling processes adjust.

### Q: How do you ensure accountability?

**A:** Multiple mechanisms:
1. **Real-time public dashboard:** Anyone can track progress
2. **Annual independent audit:** By Auditor-General
3. **Parliamentary review:** Every 2 years
4. **Community oversight:** Local implementation committees
5. **Performance-based funding:** Programs scaled up/down based on results

If a program isn't delivering 3.5x multiplier, it gets reformed or cancelled.

## ENVIRONMENTAL QUESTIONS

### Q: What's the actual emissions reduction?

**A:** By 2035:
- **Direct energy savings:** 45 Mt CO₂/year
- **Embodied carbon reduction:** 12 Mt CO₂/year
- **Waste diversion:** 8 Mt CO₂-e/year
- **Total: 65 Mt CO₂-e/year**

That's 13% of Australia's current annual emissions (500 Mt CO₂-e).

### Q: Isn't recycling batteries energy-intensive?

**A:** Yes, but:
- Recycling uses 70% less energy than virgin mining
- Recovered lithium has 50% lower carbon footprint
- Local processing avoids shipping waste overseas
- Renewable energy powers microfactories

Net benefit: 3.2 tonnes CO₂ saved per tonne of batteries recycled.

### Q: What about the environmental impact of e-bikes and EVs?

**A:** Full lifecycle analysis shows:
- **E-bike:** 22g CO₂/km (including manufacturing, electricity)
- **EV:** 80g CO₂/km (Australian grid mix)
- **Petrol car:** 251g CO₂/km

Even with current grid, EVs are 68% cleaner. With renewable energy: 90%+ reduction.

E-bikes have 97% lower emissions than cars.

## COMPARISON QUESTIONS

### Q: How does this compare to other countries?

**A:** Australia-specific advantages:
- **Solar resources:** Best in world—can power everything renewably
- **Space:** Room for distributed infrastructure
- **Research excellence:** UNSW SMaRT Centre world-leading
- **Existing schemes:** B-cycle, NatHERS building ratings

We're adapting proven models (EU circular economy, Chinese battery swapping) to Australian conditions.

### Q: Why not just wait for market forces?

**A:** Market failures exist:
- **Split incentives:** Landlords don't invest; tenants pay bills
- **Information asymmetry:** Consumers don't know best options
- **Upfront costs:** Efficiency pays back, but needs capital
- **Network effects:** E-bike lanes only work if connected

Government intervention corrects these failures, then markets take over.

### Q: What if fuel excise doesn't decline as fast?

**A:** The framework still works:
- Energy efficiency saves money regardless
- Jobs are created regardless
- Emissions reduced regardless
- Health benefits realised regardless

Fuel excise decline accelerates the case, but isn't the only justification.
